1. Introduction: The Quest for Hidden Wisdom

Muslims believe the Quran is the literal word of God revealed to Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) over 1400 years ago, and that the Hadith record his sayings, actions, and approvals. These texts address spirituality, law, morality, and natural phenomena. While their primary purpose is guidance, many verses and traditions contain descriptions of the natural world and human experience that have prompted modern readers to search for scientific or technical insight.

This report systematically reviews academic and theological literature that explores such intersections, focusing on elements that might inspire or improve artificial intelligence and other emerging technologies. We examine:

  • AI applications inspired by embryological descriptions.
  • Cosmological metaphors relevant to large‑scale simulations.

  • Systems‑thinking concepts (Tawhid, Mizan, Nur).
  • Epistemological principles for machine learning.

Our approach combines textual analysis, scholarly commentary, and critical evaluation to separate robust parallels from speculative overreach.

2. Scientific Miracles: Quranic Insights into Nature

2.1 Human Embryology and Staged Development

The Quran contains remarkably detailed references to human embryonic development, divided into stages that have been validated by modern science only in the last century.

Key verses (Surah Al‑Mu’minun 23:12–14):

We created man from a quintessence of clay. We then placed him as a drop (nutfah) in a place of settlement, firmly fixed, then We made the drop into a leech‑like structure (alaqah), then We changed the alaqah into a chewed substance (mudghah), then We made out of that mudghah bones (izam), then We clothed the bones with flesh (lahm), then We caused him to grow and come into being and attain the definitive human form.

Saadat [1] maps these stages to the germinal (nutfah), embryonic (alaqah, mudghah, izam, lahm), and fetal (nash’ah) periods recognized in embryology. The term alaqah simultaneously means “leech,” “suspended thing,” and “blood clot”—all accurately describing the embryo’s appearance and attachment at 15–24 days. “Mudghah” denotes a “chewed substance,” reflecting somite patterns resembling teeth marks. The sequential “then” (fa…) indicates rapid succession of events that modern science confirms.

AI Relevance: These stage‑wise transformations echo concepts in developmental AI and curriculum learning. Just as the embryo proceeds through discrete, well‑ordered phases, AI systems could benefit from staged training regimes, where foundational representations (e.g., sensory‑motor loops) are solidified before introducing higher‑level reasoning. The precision of the Quranic descriptions also suggests that natural computation employs stepwise refinement—a principle already present in deep neural network architectures (e.g., ResNet blocks). More speculative: the rapid “fa” conjunction might inspire modules with accelerated information flow, while “thumma” (then) could signal slower, integrative phases analogous to consolidation periods in continual learning.

2.2 Cosmology and the Universe

Suparno’s comprehensive study [2] identifies ten scientific miracles in the Quran related to cosmology and physics:

  • Singularity — The heavens and earth were initially a single connected mass, then split apart (Quran 21:30). This aligns with the Big Bang singularity.
  • Expanding Universe — “We are expanding the heavens” (51:47) indicates cosmic expansion, discovered by Hubble only in the 20th century.
  • Big Crunch — References to the heavens being rolled up (21:104) suggest a future recollapse.
  • Origin of Water and Iron — Iron is said to be sent down from the sky (57:25); water is described as a lifegiving substance created with purpose (23:45). Modern astronomy confirms that heavy elements originate from supernovae and water-bearing comets.
  • Protection by the Sky — The atmosphere shields life from harmful radiation and meteoroids (21:32).
  • Living Things in the Sky — References to birds’ flight being enabled by the structural integrity of the sky (67:19) hint at ecological dependencies.

AI Relevance: The Quran’s cosmic perspective can inspire multi‑scale modeling. A singular origin followed by differentiation parallels initialization‑then‑expansion strategies in generative models (e.g., diffusion). The recurring theme of expansion (51:47) resembles attention mechanisms that dynamically enlarge receptive fields. The Big Crunch might inform theoretical work on model compression or cyclic universes of representation. The origin of elements suggests that complex structures (like neural networks) are formed from simpler, “sent‑down” components, echoing modular design where sophisticated capabilities emerge from combining primitive operations.

2.3 Critical Perspective: Avoiding Over‑Interpretation

Taner Edis [3] reminds us that “Quran‑science” often mirrors Christian creationist approaches, involving strained readings and confirmation bias. Many alleged “miracles” rely on vague translations or selective emphasis, while the Quran’s own cosmology (e.g., seven heavens, flat earth) aligns with 7th‑centuryNear Eastern mythology. For instance, the expanding‑universe claim depends on a dubious translation of 51:47 (“we are the Lord of power and expanse”) and conflicts with other verses describing God stretching the earth.

Implication for AI Research: When mining religious texts for technological ideas, we must maintain high methodological standards. Metaphors should be clearly distinguished from literal claims, and interdisciplinary teams (including domain scientists and Islamic scholars) should vet interpretations. This ensures that AI innovations inspired by sacred texts are robust, ethically sound, and respectful of theological integrity.

3. Ethical and Legal Frameworks for AI from Islamic Jurisprudence

Modern AI systems raise profound ethical questions: How should autonomous decisions be governed? What values should be embedded? Islamic jurisprudence (fiqh) offers a rich, principle‑based methodology that can inform AI ethics.

3.1 Maqāṣid al‑Sharīʿah: The Objectives of Islamic Law

Siddique & Rauf [4] explain that maqāṣid al‑sharīʿah are higher objectives derived from the Quran and Sunnah. The classical five necessities (preservation of life, intellect, religion, lineage, and property) provide a value hierarchy that can shape AI development. For example:

  • Life — AI medical diagnostics should prioritize patient survival and well‑being.
  • Intellect — Surveillance or recommendation algorithms must not impair rational autonomy.
  • Religion — Systems interacting with believers should respect worship practices and avoid blasphemous outputs.
  • Lineage — Genetic AI applications must consider impacts on inheritance and family structures.
  • Property — Data ownership and algorithmic monetization should be just.

These objectives can be translated into measurable AI constraints (e.g., via multi‑objective optimization) or incorporated into alignment objectives.

3.2 Qiyās, Istiḥsān, and Maslaḥah

Classical jurists developed tools for legal reasoning when explicit texts are silent:

  • Qiyās (analogical reasoning) maps a new case to a known ruling based on effective cause (ʿillah). In AI, this suggests building analogical reasoning modules that can generalize from known ethical scenarios to novel ones (e.g., “Is this AI‑driven credit scoring analogous to usury?”).
  • Istiḥsān (juristic preference) allows overriding strict analogy for equity. This could guide AI to sometimes relax rules for greater fairness, akin to “ethical override” mechanisms.
  • Maslaḥah (public interest) permits actions that serve the common good. AI governance could incorporate public‑interest metrics beyond individual utility.

3.3 Applications in Modern AI

The authors discuss AI‑driven medical diagnostics, genetic editing, end‑of‑life decisions, mass surveillance, and machine‑learning fairness. For instance, predictive policing algorithms can be evaluated against maslaḥah to ensure they protect communities without unjust profiling. AI transparency requirements align with the Islamic principle of shura (consultation). The framework encourages developers to consider not only technical performance but also alignment with maqāṣid.

4. Systems Thinking and Architectural Metaphors

4.1 Tawhid — Unity as a Design Principle

The core Islamic declaration, the Shahada, affirms the absolute Oneness of God (Tawhid). This concept extends beyond monotheism to a holistic worldview where all existence is an interconnected whole under a single sovereignty [9]. In systems thinking, Tawhid suggests that an intelligent system—whether an organization or an AI—should strive for integration rather than fragmented modules. While modularity is useful, ultimate coherence emerges only when parts jointly serve a unified purpose.

Implications for AI:

  • Unified Ontologies — Knowledge graphs and world models should form a consistent, non‑contradictory structure analogous to the cosmic unity.
  • Singular Objective Functions — Rather than balancing multiple disjoint rewards, AI should seek a meta‑objective that subsumes subordinate goals without conflict (akin to tawhid as the ultimate integrating principle).
  • Holistic Evaluation — System‑level impacts (environmental, societal, ethical) must be assessed, not just task performance.

Al‑Jayyousi et al. [5] show how Tawhid underpins sustainable development within Islamic worldviews, teaching that all creation is a trust (amanah) from the One, demanding responsible stewardship—a principle that can guide AI alignment toward long‑term human flourishing.

4.2 Mizan — Balance and Fairness

The Quran frequently mentions mizan (balance) as a divine attribute and a natural law: “He has set up the balance (mizan)…” (Quran 55:7–9). This balance governs both physical phenomena (e.g., orbital mechanics) and moral order (justice, proportionality).

AI Relevance:

  • Algorithmic Fairness — Mizan demands equitable treatment across groups. Statistical parity, equalized odds, and other fairness metrics can be seen as technical instantiations of this principle.
  • Multi‑Objective Optimization — Balancing competing objectives (accuracy vs. privacy, efficiency vs. robustness) finds a parallel in maintaining cosmic and moral equilibrium.
  • Regularization — Techniques that penalize extreme weights (e.g., weight decay) mirror the Quranic injunction against excess (Islah).

Incorporating mizan into AI design encourages humility: systems should not seek absolute domination but harmonious coexistence with humans and the environment.

Source: Quran 55:7–9 [8]

4.3 Nur — Light as Knowledge and Guidance

“Allah is the Light of the heavens and the earth” (Quran 24:35). Nur (light) symbolizes divine guidance, enlightenment, and revelation. In cognitive science, light makes things visible; in AI, information illuminates patterns.

AI Relevance:

  • Attention Mechanisms — Transformers allocate “attention” like a spotlight, highlighting relevant input regions. This mirrors nur as selective illumination.
  • Explainable AI — Post‑hoc explanations that make model decisions transparent can be viewed as generating “light” on otherwise opaque reasoning.
  • Generative Models — Diffusion models literally start from random noise and gradually “illuminate” data. The metaphor of emerging from darkness to light is powerful.
  • Guided Search — Heuristics that steer exploration reflect the idea of divine light guiding the seeker.

Thus nur suggests that AI should not only process data but also illuminate the underlying truths for human understanding.

5. Epistemology of Knowledge: Hadith and AI Learning

Islamic epistemology distinguishes between ‘ilm (knowledge) and hikmah (wisdom). Knowledge is the acquisition of facts; wisdom applies them rightly. Several hadiths extol the pursuit of knowledge:

“Whoever treads a path in search of knowledge, Allah will make easy for him the path to Paradise.” — Sahih Muslim 2699a [6]

Other traditions state: “Seek knowledge from the cradle to the grave” (though its authenticity is debated) and “The seeking of knowledge is obligatory upon every Muslim” (Ibn Majah). These emphasize lifelong, communal, and even obligatory learning.

AI Connections

  • Lifelong Learning — AI systems should continuously acquire new knowledge without catastrophic forgetting, mirroring the Islamic ideal of never‑ending learning.
  • Teaching and Transmission — The hadith about gathering to read and teach the Quran suggests that learning is a social activity. AI can benefit from mechanisms that share knowledge between agents (e.g., federated learning, knowledge distillation).
  • Wisdom (Hikmah) — The ultimate goal is not raw information but wise application. AI alignment research seeks to imbue systems with practical wisdom—the ability to act in accordance with human values across diverse contexts.
  • Ethical Motivation — The hadith ties the pursuit of knowledge to spiritual reward. For AI, we might design incentives that align learning with beneficial outcomes (e.g., curiosity driven by intrinsic value alignment).
